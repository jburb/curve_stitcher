Squarus explores polyomino families by square count, then studies how those pieces can be arranged using different layout strategies.

Polyominoes have long been a rich source of mathematical puzzles. They became a worldwide cultural phenomenon when Alexey Pajitnov created Tetris, built around the seven tetrominoes.

Tetris is often discussed as a real-time puzzle game, but many of its placement questions can also be modeled as exact cover style problems: can a set of pieces satisfy a set of occupancy constraints exactly once, without overlap or gaps?

In Squarus, changing order, contact style, sequence, and layout lets you move between puzzle intuition and combinatorial structure. The result is both playful and mathematical: a study of how local piece rules create global form.
